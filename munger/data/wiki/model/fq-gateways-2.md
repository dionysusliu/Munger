# FQ Gateways

## Overview

**FQ Gateways** (Frequently Questioned Gateways) is a mental model for decision-making that helps identify and prioritize the most critical assumptions, uncertainties, or "gates" in any process, project, or plan. The model suggests that by focusing on the points most likely to be challenged or questioned—and addressing them proactively—you can prevent bottlenecks, reduce friction, and accelerate progress.

The term "gateway" here refers to a checkpoint or barrier that must be passed before proceeding further. An "FQ Gateway" is one that will inevitably face scrutiny, doubt, or resistance from stakeholders, reviewers, or even yourself.

## Core Principles

1. **Identify the Most Questioned Gates** – Not all barriers are equal. Some will naturally attract more skepticism or require more evidence to pass.
2. **Address Before They're Asked** – Preemptively answer the hardest questions to build credibility and momentum.
3. **Prioritize by Impact** – Focus on gates that, if blocked, would halt or derail the entire process.
4. **Iterate and Refine** – As you pass one gateway, new FQ Gateways may emerge.

## How It Works

The model follows a simple three-step process:

1. **Map the Process** – Outline the key stages, decisions, or approvals in your plan.
2. **Identify FQ Gateways** – For each stage, ask: "What is the single most likely point of contention, doubt, or failure?"
3. **Prepare and Pass** – Gather evidence, build arguments, or adjust your approach to satisfy the gateway criteria before encountering it.

## Examples

### Example 1: Product Launch

| Stage | FQ Gateway | Preparation |
|-------|------------|-------------|
| Concept | "Does anyone actually need this?" | Market research, user interviews, problem validation |
| Design | "Is this usable?" | Prototype testing, usability studies |
| Development | "Is it secure?" | Security audit, penetration testing |
| Launch | "Will people pay?" | Pricing strategy, beta sales, willingness-to-pay surveys |

### Example 2: Career Decision

| Decision Point | FQ Gateway | Preparation |
|----------------|------------|-------------|
| Changing jobs | "Am I qualified?" | Skill gap analysis, portfolio, references |
| Asking for promotion | "What's my measurable impact?" | Quantified achievements, performance data |
| Starting a side business | "Can I afford the risk?" | Financial runway, minimum viable product, lean approach |

### Example 3: Academic Research

| Phase | FQ Gateway | Preparation |
|-------|------------|-------------|
| Proposal | "Is this novel?" | Literature review, gap analysis |
| Methodology | "Is this valid?" | Peer review, pilot study, statistical power analysis |
| Results | "Are these significant?" | Effect size, confidence intervals, replication |
| Publication | "Does this advance the field?" | Clear implications, practical applications, limitations addressed |

## Applications

### Project Management
- Identify the approval or review stage most likely to cause delays
- Prepare comprehensive documentation for that specific gateway
- Use the model to sequence tasks: tackle FQ Gateways early

### Negotiation
- Anticipate the objections your counterpart will raise
- Prepare counterarguments or concessions for each FQ Gateway
- Address the most difficult points first to build trust

### Personal Growth
- Identify the beliefs or habits most likely to prevent you from achieving a goal
- Focus on changing those specific FQ Gateways rather than everything at once
- Example: If you want to exercise more, the FQ Gateway might be "I don't have time" → schedule it first

### Decision-Making
- When evaluating options, list the FQ Gateways for each
- Compare which option has the most passable gateways
- Choose the path with the fewest high-risk uncertainties

## Related Models

- [[Pre-Mortem]] – Imagine a future failure to identify risks in advance
- [[First Principles Thinking]] – Break down assumptions to their most basic truths
- [[Inversion]] – Consider the opposite of what you want to identify obstacles
- [[Occam's Razor]] – Simpler explanations are more likely to be correct (and less likely to be questioned)

## Limitations

- **Over-preparation**: It's possible to spend too much time on one gateway while neglecting others
- **False confidence**: Passing an FQ Gateway doesn't guarantee success—other unforeseen barriers may arise
- **Bias in identification**: You might misjudge which gateways will actually be questioned most
- **Context dependence**: What's an FQ Gateway in one situation may be trivial in another

## Summary

FQ Gateways is a mental model that helps you focus your limited time and energy on the points of highest friction. By identifying and addressing the most frequently questioned barriers in advance, you can move through projects, decisions, and negotiations with greater speed and confidence. The model is especially useful in environments where approval, consensus, or buy-in is required, but it applies broadly to any situation where assumptions need validation.
