# NW (Net Worth)

## Definition
**NW** (abbreviation for **Net Worth**) is a financial metric representing the value of an individual, organization, or entity’s assets minus its liabilities. It is a snapshot of financial health and is used to measure wealth, solvency, and long-term progress toward financial goals.

**Formula:**  
`Net Worth = Total Assets – Total Liabilities`

## Examples
- **Personal Net Worth:**  
  A person owns a house worth $300,000 (asset), has a mortgage of $200,000 (liability), a car worth $15,000 (asset), and $5,000 in credit card debt (liability). Their net worth is $300,000 + $15,000 – $200,000 – $5,000 = **$110,000**.

- **Business Net Worth (Shareholder Equity):**  
  A company has $1M in cash, $2M in inventory, and $500K in accounts receivable (total assets = $3.5M). It owes $1M in loans and $200K in accounts payable (total liabilities = $1.2M). Net worth = $3.5M – $1.2M = **$2.3M**.

- **Negative Net Worth:**  
  If an individual has $50,000 in student loans and only $10,000 in savings, their net worth is –$40,000. This is common early in life but can be reversed over time.

## Related Mental Models
- **[[Balance Sheet]]** – The fundamental framework that organizes assets and liabilities to calculate net worth.
- **[[Compound Interest]]** – A key driver of net worth growth when assets are invested and reinvested.
- **[[Opportunity Cost]]** – Every financial decision (spending, saving, investing) alters net worth, requiring trade‑offs.
- **[[Sunk Cost]]** – Past spending should not influence future net-worth decisions; focus on current value.
- **[[Leverage]]** – Using borrowed money (liabilities) to acquire assets can amplify net worth gains or losses.
- **[[Liquidity]]** – The ease of converting assets to cash affects net worth stability and risk.
