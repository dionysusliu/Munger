# NW

**NW** is an abbreviation that can refer to multiple concepts depending on context. In the domain of mental models, systems thinking, and decision-making, **NW** most commonly stands for **"Nash Equilibrium"** (game theory) or **"Network"** (systems theory). This page focuses on the **Nash Equilibrium** interpretation, as it is a foundational mental model for understanding strategic interactions.

## Definition

**Nash Equilibrium** (named after mathematician John Nash) is a concept in game theory where each player in a strategic interaction chooses the best possible strategy for themselves, given the strategies chosen by all other players. In a Nash Equilibrium, no player has an incentive to unilaterally change their strategy, because doing so would not improve their outcome.

In simpler terms: it is a stable state of a system where everyone is doing the best they can, given what everyone else is doing.

## Key Characteristics

- **Mutual best response**: Each player's strategy is optimal, assuming the others' strategies are fixed.
- **No unilateral incentive**: No single player can benefit by changing their own strategy alone.
- **Not necessarily optimal for the group**: The equilibrium may be suboptimal for all players collectively (e.g., the Prisoner's Dilemma).
- **Can be multiple**: A game can have zero, one, or many Nash Equilibria.

## Examples

### 1. The Prisoner's Dilemma
Two criminals are arrested and interrogated separately. Each can either **confess** or **remain silent**.
- If both remain silent, they each get 1 year.
- If one confesses and the other remains silent, the confessor goes free, the other gets 10 years.
- If both confess, they each get 5 years.

The Nash Equilibrium is **both confess**, because each prisoner, knowing the other's likely choice, minimizes their own risk. This is a classic example of a suboptimal collective outcome.

### 2. Traffic Flow
Drivers choose between two routes: a highway (fast but congested) and a side road (slow but less traffic). If everyone chooses the highway, it becomes congested and slow. If everyone chooses the side road, it becomes congested too. The Nash Equilibrium occurs when drivers distribute themselves such that travel times on both routes are equal—no single driver can improve their time by switching.

### 3. Market Pricing
Two competing coffee shops in a town. If both charge $3, they split customers. If one drops to $2.50, they attract more customers, but the other may follow. The Nash Equilibrium is often a price war where both end up at a low price, even though both would prefer a higher price if they could coordinate.

## Related Mental Models

- [[Prisoner's Dilemma]] – A specific game that illustrates the tension between individual and collective rationality.
- [[Tragedy of the Commons]] – A situation where individuals acting in their own self-interest deplete a shared resource, leading to a suboptimal Nash Equilibrium.
- [[Zero-Sum Game]] – A game where one player's gain is another's loss; Nash Equilibria in zero-sum games often involve mixed strategies.
- [[Coordination Game]] – A game where players benefit from choosing the same strategy; multiple Nash Equilibria may exist.
- [[Minimax Theorem]] – A related concept in zero-sum games, where players minimize their maximum possible loss.
- [[Game Theory]] – The broader mathematical framework that includes Nash Equilibrium.
- [[Incentive Design]] – How to structure rules to shift Nash Equilibria toward more desirable outcomes.
- [[Bounded Rationality]] – Real-world limitations that prevent players from always reaching Nash Equilibrium.

## Practical Applications

- **Business strategy**: Anticipating competitor moves in pricing, product launches, or advertising.
- **Public policy**: Designing regulations (e.g., carbon taxes) to shift equilibria toward cooperation.
- **Negotiation**: Understanding when a deal is stable and when one party has an incentive to defect.
- **Artificial intelligence**: Multi-agent systems often use Nash Equilibrium concepts for coordination and competition.

## Limitations

- Assumes **rationality** and **common knowledge** of rationality, which rarely holds in real life.
- Does not account for **learning**, **emotion**, or **social norms**.
- May be **computationally intractable** in complex games with many players or strategies.
- **Multiple equilibria** can make prediction difficult without additional assumptions (e.g., focal points).
