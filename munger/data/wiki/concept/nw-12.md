# NW (Network)

The **NW** (Network) mental model is a way of thinking about systems as collections of interconnected nodes (people, computers, ideas, etc.) and the relationships between them. It emphasizes patterns of connectivity, flow, and influence rather than isolated components. By applying a network lens, you can better understand how information spreads, power is distributed, and behaviors emerge in complex environments.

## Definition

A **network** is any structure composed of:

- **Nodes** — the individual entities (e.g., people, organizations, web pages, neurons)
- **Edges** — the connections or relationships between nodes (e.g., friendships, citations, hyperlinks, synapses)

The mental model itself involves analyzing the **topology** (shape), **dynamics** (how things move through the network), and **emergent properties** (behaviors that arise from the whole) of such systems.

Key concepts within the NW model include:

- **Centrality** — some nodes are more important or influential than others (see [[Centrality]])
- **Clusters** — groups of densely interconnected nodes (see [[Clustering Coefficient]])
- **Hubs** — high-degree nodes that act as connectors (see [[Hub (Network)]])
- **Weak Ties** — bridges between otherwise separated clusters (see [[Weak Ties]])
- **Small-world property** — any two nodes are typically just a few hops apart (see [[Six Degrees of Separation]])

## Examples

1. **Social Networks**  
   On platforms like Twitter or LinkedIn, your reach and influence depend on your position in the network. A large follower count (high degree) or connections to diverse communities (bridge roles) can amplify your message.

2. **Epidemiology**  
   Disease spread is a classic network phenomenon. “Superspreaders” are high-centrality nodes that can infect many others, while quarantine targets network edges to break transmission paths.

3. **The World Wide Web**  
   Google’s PageRank algorithm leverages the network of hyperlinks. Pages with many incoming links (links are edges) are considered more authoritative and rank higher.

4. **Business Ecosystems**  
   A company’s suppliers, partners, and customers form a network. Disruption to a single key supplier (hub) can cascade through the entire system.

5. **Neural Networks**  
   Both biological and artificial neural networks rely on connections (synapses/weights) between neurons/nodes to process information. The network structure determines learning capacity.

## Related Mental Models

- [[Metcalfe’s Law]] – The value of a network grows as the square of the number of its nodes. Directly quantifies network effects.
- [[Network Effects]] – The phenomenon where a product or service becomes more valuable as more people use it.
- [[Dunbar’s Number]] – A cognitive limit on the number of stable social relationships a human can maintain, reflecting network constraints.
- [[Path Dependence]] – Historical decisions in network formation can lock in certain structures (e.g., QWERTY keyboard as a “network” of habits).
- [[Emergence]] – Complex global patterns arise from simple local rules of connection and interaction.
- [[Feedback Loops]] – Networks often contain reinforcing or balancing feedback (e.g., viral growth in a social network).
- [[Strength of Weak Ties]] – Mark Granovetter’s idea that weak ties (acquaintances) are often more valuable than strong ties for spreading novel information.

> [!tip] Using the NW mental model in decision-making
> Before intervening in any system, map its network: Who are the key nodes? Where are the bottlenecks? Which ties are redundant? This perspective helps you identify leverage points and anticipate unintended consequences.
