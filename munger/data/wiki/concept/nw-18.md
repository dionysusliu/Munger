# NW (Narrow World)

**Definition**: The **Narrow World (NW)** mental model describes the cognitive bias in which individuals base their understanding of reality on a limited, often unrepresentative, set of personal experiences or information sources. This over‑reliance on a narrow slice of data leads to overgeneralization, false consensus, and systematically flawed decisions.

## Characteristics
- **Limited Sampling**: Drawing conclusions from a small, non‑random subset of the full picture.
- **Local Optimisation**: Assuming that what works in one context will work in all contexts.
- **Tunnel Vision**: Ignoring information that falls outside one’s immediate awareness or comfort zone.

## Examples
- **Tech Bubble**: A software engineer who interacts only with technically savvy colleagues assumes that all users are equally proficient, leading to poor product design.
- **Local News Effect**: A person who watches a daily local news broadcast believes crime is rising in their country, despite national statistics showing a decline.
- **Expert Overconfidence**: A specialist in a narrow field (e.g., a surgeon) may overestimate their ability to solve problems in unrelated domains (medicine’s version of the [[Dunning–Kruger Effect]]).

## Related Mental Models
- **[[Availability Heuristic]]** – Judging probability by how easily examples come to mind; the NW model often amplifies this heuristic.
- **[[Confirmation Bias]]** – Seeking and remembering information that confirms existing beliefs, which further narrows one’s world.
- **[[Streetlight Effect]]** – Looking for answers only where it is easy to see, rather than where the truth might lie.
- **[[Map is Not the Territory]]** – Treating simplified mental models as if they were complete realities.
- **[[Blind Spot Bias]]** – Recognising narrowness in others while being blind to it in oneself.

## Counter‑Strategies
1. **Seek Disconfirming Evidence**: Actively look for data that challenges your current view.
2. **Diversify Inputs**: Consume information from different fields, geographies, and cultures.
3. **Use Reference Class Forecasting**: Compare your situation with a broad, relevant set of outcomes (see [[Outside View]]).
4. **Calibrate Confidence**: Regularly question how much you really know by testing predictions.
