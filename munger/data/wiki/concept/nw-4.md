# NW

## Definition

**NW** is an abbreviation most commonly used to refer to **Networking**—the practice of interconnecting systems, devices, or individuals to enable communication, resource sharing, and collaboration. In a technological context, NW encompasses the hardware, protocols, and topologies that allow computers and other devices to exchange data. As a mental model, NW represents the structure and dynamics of interconnected elements, whether they are machines, people, or abstract nodes.

Examples include:

- **Computer networks**: Local Area Networks (LANs), Wide Area Networks (WANs), and the Internet.
- **Social networks**: Relationships and interactions among individuals or groups.
- **Biological networks**: Neural networks, food webs, and gene regulatory networks.

## Core Concepts

- **Nodes and Edges**: Any network consists of entities (nodes) and the connections between them (edges).  
- **Topology**: The physical or logical arrangement of nodes (e.g., star, mesh, bus, ring).  
- **Protocols**: Agreed-upon rules for communication (e.g., TCP/IP, HTTP).  
- **Network Effects**: The phenomenon where the value of a network increases as more nodes join (see [[Metcalfe's Law]]).

## Examples

### Example 1: File Sharing on a Local Network
- A printer connected via Wi‑Fi to multiple computers in an office. Each computer can send print jobs because the NW interface routes the data correctly.

### Example 2: Social Media Platform
- Users (nodes) follow each other (edges). The platform’s value grows as more people join, creating a [[Network Effect]].

### Example 3: The Internet as a Network of Networks
- Millions of smaller networks (ISPs, corporate LANs) interconnect using the global IP addressing system, enabling worldwide communication.

## Related Mental Models

- [[Graph Theory]] – The mathematical foundation for modeling networks with nodes and edges.  
- [[Metcalfe's Law]] – States that the value of a network is proportional to the square of the number of users.  
- [[Small World Phenomenon]] – The idea that most nodes in a large network can be reached through a short chain of acquaintances (six degrees of separation).  
- [[Network Effects]] – The reinforcing feedback loop that makes larger networks more attractive.  
- [[Scale-free Networks]] – Networks where a few nodes (hubs) have many connections, while most have few (e.g., the World Wide Web).  
- [[Six Degrees of Separation]] – A social network model suggesting everyone is within about six connections of any other person.
