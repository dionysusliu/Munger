# NW (Network)

**NW** is a shorthand for **network** — a structure composed of interconnected nodes (entities) and edges (relationships). Networks are foundational to understanding complex systems, from social interactions and biological processes to technological infrastructure and economic markets. As a mental model, thinking in terms of networks helps reveal patterns of influence, flow, and emergence that are invisible when focusing on individual components alone.

## Definition

A network is any collection of **nodes** (also called vertices) and **edges** (links or connections) that represent relationships between those nodes. The topology of a network — how nodes are arranged and connected — determines how information, resources, or influence travel through the system. Key properties include:

- **Degree**: The number of connections a node has.
- **Centrality**: Measures of a node’s importance (e.g., degree centrality, betweenness centrality).
- **Clustering**: How tightly nodes form local groups.
- **Path length**: The average number of steps between any two nodes.

Networks can be **directed** (edges have a direction, e.g., Twitter follows) or **undirected** (edges are symmetric, e.g., Facebook friendships), **weighted** (edges have strengths) or **binary** (connected/not connected), and can exhibit different degree distributions (e.g., scale‑free, random, small‑world).

## Examples

- **Social networks**: People connected by friendships, professional ties, or communication. Understanding network structure explains phenomena like viral trends, information cascades, and the spread of behaviors.
- **Transportation networks**: Roads, railways, flight routes. Hubs like major airports become critical bottlenecks or failure points.
- **Computer networks**: The internet, local area networks, or blockchain nodes. The structure affects latency, resilience, and security.
- **Neural networks**: Biological brains or artificial neural networks where neurons (nodes) and synapses (edges) process information.
- **Economic networks**: Supply chains, trade relationships, or interlocking directorates. The 2008 financial crisis was amplified by hidden network dependencies.

## Related Mental Models

- **[[Network Effects]]**: The value of a product or service increases as more people use it. Classic examples: telephones, social media platforms.
- **[[Metcalfe’s Law]]**: The value of a network is proportional to the square of the number of users (n²).
- **[[Small World Phenomenon]]** (Six Degrees of Separation): Most nodes can be reached from any other node through a surprisingly short chain of acquaintances.
- **[[Hub and Spoke]]**: A network topology where a central node (hub) connects to many peripheral nodes (spokes). Common in airlines, logistics, and organizational hierarchies.
- **[[Weak Ties]]**: Weak, infrequent connections often provide more novel information than strong ties, as popularized by Mark Granovetter.
- **[[Scale‑Free Networks]]**: Networks where a few hubs have many connections while most nodes have few. These are vulnerable to targeted attacks on hubs but robust to random failures.
- **[[Centrality]]**: Metrics that identify the most influential or strategically positioned nodes in a network.
- **[[Percolation Threshold]]**: The critical point at which a network becomes connected enough for a property (e.g., a disease or idea) to spread globally.
- **[[Buridan’s Ass]]** (applied to networks): The paralysis that can occur when a node with many conflicting connections becomes overloaded with choices.
