# The Nine Windows (9Ws)

**The Nine Windows** (also known as *9 Windows* or *Systematic Inventive Thinking Windows*) is a mental model used for **problem-solving, innovation, and systems thinking**. It expands the perspective of a problem or system across three **time horizons** (Past, Present, Future) and three **levels of abstraction** (Super-system, System, Sub-system). This creates a 3x3 grid (9 "windows") that forces the thinker to consider factors that are usually ignored.

## Core Definition

The model is based on the principle that a single system (the "System") does not exist in a vacuum. It is composed of smaller parts (Sub-system) and exists within a larger environment (Super-system). Furthermore, this arrangement is not static; it has a history and a future trajectory.

By systematically examining all nine windows, you can uncover hidden constraints, identify root causes, foresee unintended consequences, and discover novel opportunities for innovation.

## The 9-Window Grid

The model is visualized as a 3x3 matrix:

| | **Past** | **Present** | **Future** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Super-system** | Past Super-system | Current Super-system | Future Super-system |
| **System** | Past System | **Current Problem/System** | Future System |
| **Sub-system** | Past Sub-system | Current Sub-system | Future Sub-system |

- **System:** The core entity you are analyzing (e.g., a car, a business process, a human body).
- **Sub-system:** The parts that make up the System (e.g., engine, wheels; employees, software; organs, cells).
- **Super-system:** The larger environment the System is part of (e.g., the city traffic system; the industry market; the ecosystem).
- **Time:** The state of each of these levels in the past (history), present (current state), and future (predicted or desired state).

## How to Use It

1.  **Define the System:** Clearly state the core problem or object in the "Present System" cell.
2.  **Fill the Present Column:** Describe the current Super-system and Sub-system.
3.  **Explore the Past:** How did the System, Sub-system, and Super-system look before? What was the root cause or previous state?
4.  **Project the Future:** What will the System, Sub-system, and Super-system look like in the future? Consider trends, decay, and evolution.
5.  **Look for Insights:** The most valuable insights often come from the corners of the grid (e.g., Past Super-system, Future Sub-system). Ask: "What constraint in the Past Super-system no longer exists?" or "What resource in the Future Sub-system can we prepare for now?"

## Examples

### Example 1: A Coffee Cup (Product Design)

- **Problem:** The coffee gets cold too quickly.
- **System:** The ceramic coffee cup.
- **Sub-system:** The ceramic material, the handle.
- **Super-system:** The office desk, the user, the room temperature.

**Application:**
- **Past Super-system:** Offices were colder (less insulation).
- **Future Sub-system:** New materials (aerogel, phase-change materials).
- **Insight:** Instead of just making the cup thicker (system), consider the **Future Super-system** (user might be walking) and the **Past Sub-system** (the cup used to have a lid). **Solution:** A self-heating cup or a better insulating lid for mobile use.

### Example 2: A Failing Business Process (Management)

- **Problem:** Sales team is missing targets.
- **System:** The sales team.
- **Sub-system:** Sales reps, CRM software, commission structure.
- **Super-system:** The company, the market, the economy.

**Application:**
- **Past Sub-system:** The old CRM was simpler.
- **Future Super-system:** AI-powered competitors are entering the market.
- **Insight:** The problem isn't the reps (system). The **Past Super-system** had less competition, and the **Future Sub-system** (commission structure) doesn't reward the new skills needed. **Solution:** Change the commission structure to reward AI-tool usage and relationship building, not just cold calls.

## Related Mental Models

- [[Second-Order Thinking]]: The "Future" column of the 9 Windows is a structured way to practice second-order thinking.
- [[Inversion]]: Use the 9 Windows to invert the problem by asking "What would make the Future Super-system worse?".
- [[Occam's Razor]]: The 9 Windows can help you find the simplest explanation by showing that the root cause is often in a different window than the symptom.
- [[Thought Experiment]]: The "Future" windows are essentially structured thought experiments.
- [[Root Cause Analysis]]: The "Past" column is a powerful tool for tracing a problem back to its origin, often revealing a cause in the Super-system or Sub-system that was overlooked.

## Limitations

- **Complexity:** For very simple problems, the 9x9 grid can feel overly bureaucratic.
- **Bias:** The quality of the output depends entirely on the quality of the information you put into each window. Garbage in, garbage out.
- **Paralysis by Analysis:** It is possible to get lost in exploring every possible future state. The model is a tool for *direction*, not a crystal ball.
