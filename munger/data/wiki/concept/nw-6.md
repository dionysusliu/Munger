# NW

**NW** is an abbreviation that can refer to several distinct concepts depending on context. In the domain of mental models, systems thinking, and cognitive frameworks, the most common interpretations are:

- **[[Nash Equilibrium]]** (game theory)
- **[[Network Effects]]** (economics & technology)
- **[[Normalcy Bias]]** (psychology & decision-making)
- **[[Nudge Theory]]** (behavioral economics)

This page focuses on the most widely referenced mental model associated with "NW": **Nash Equilibrium**.

---

## Definition

**Nash Equilibrium** (named after mathematician John Nash) is a concept in game theory where each player in a strategic interaction chooses the best possible strategy given the strategies chosen by all other players. In a Nash Equilibrium, no player has an incentive to unilaterally change their strategy, because doing so would not improve their outcome.

Key characteristics:
- **Self-enforcing**: Once reached, no player wants to deviate.
- **Non-cooperative**: Players act independently, without collusion.
- **May not be optimal**: The equilibrium can be suboptimal for all players (e.g., the [[Prisoner's Dilemma]]).

---

## Examples

### 1. The Prisoner's Dilemma
Two suspects are arrested and interrogated separately. Each can either confess or remain silent. The Nash Equilibrium is for both to confess, even though mutual silence would yield a better collective outcome.

| | Suspect B stays silent | Suspect B confesses |
|---|---|---|
| Suspect A stays silent | 1 year each | A: 10 years, B: 0 years |
| Suspect A confesses | A: 0 years, B: 10 years | A: 5 years, B: 5 years |

The dominant strategy for each is to confess → Nash Equilibrium = (confess, confess).

### 2. Traffic Flow
Drivers choose lanes to minimize travel time. If everyone chooses the same lane, congestion occurs. The Nash Equilibrium emerges when drivers distribute themselves such that no single driver can switch lanes and arrive faster.

### 3. Market Pricing
Two competing firms set prices. If both set high prices, profits are high. But each firm has an incentive to undercut the other. The Nash Equilibrium often results in both setting moderate or low prices, even though collusion would be more profitable (but illegal).

## Related Mental Models

- **[[Prisoner's Dilemma]]** – A classic game that illustrates how Nash Equilibrium can lead to suboptimal outcomes.
- **[[Tragedy of the Commons]]** – A scenario where individual rational choices (Nash Equilibrium) deplete a shared resource.
- **[[Zero-Sum Game]]** – A game where one player's gain is another's loss; Nash Equilibrium still applies but outcomes are strictly competitive.
- **[[Coordination Game]]** – A game where players benefit from choosing the same strategy; multiple Nash Equilibria may exist.
- **[[Dominant Strategy]]** – A strategy that is best regardless of what others do; a Nash Equilibrium exists when all players have dominant strategies.
- **[[Game Theory]]** – The broader mathematical framework that includes Nash Equilibrium.
- **[[Bounded Rationality]]** – A limitation that affects real-world players' ability to find Nash Equilibria.
- **[[Invisible Hand]]** – Adam Smith's concept of self-interest leading to collective benefit, which can be modeled using Nash Equilibrium in competitive markets.

---

## Practical Applications

- **Economics**: Predicting market outcomes, auction design, oligopoly pricing.
- **Politics**: Analyzing voting behavior, international treaties, arms races.
- **Biology**: Evolutionary stable strategies (ESS) in animal behavior.
- **Computer Science**: Algorithm design for multi-agent systems, network routing.
- **Everyday Life**: Negotiations, traffic decisions, social norms.

---

## Limitations & Criticisms

- **Assumes rationality**: Real humans often act irrationally or with limited information.
- **Multiple equilibria**: Some games have many Nash Equilibria, making prediction difficult.
- **Static analysis**: Does not account for learning, adaptation, or repeated interactions (see [[Repeated Games]]).
- **Coordination failure**: Players may get stuck in a suboptimal equilibrium.
