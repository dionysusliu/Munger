## Nash Equilibrium (NW)

A **Nash Equilibrium** (often abbreviated as **NW** in game theory notation) is a solution concept in a non-cooperative game involving two or more players. It is a state of play where no player has an incentive to unilaterally change their strategy, given the strategies of the other players. In simpler terms, it is a situation where everyone is doing the best they can, given what everyone else is doing.

The concept is named after the mathematician John Nash, who introduced it in 1950.

### Definition

A set of strategies (one for each player) constitutes a **Nash Equilibrium** if, for every player, their chosen strategy is the best response to the strategies of all other players.

- **Key Characteristic:** It is a stable state. No player can benefit by changing their own strategy *alone*.
- **Self-Consistency:** Each player's choice is a rational response to the choices of the others.
- **Non-Cooperative:** The equilibrium arises from individual self-interest without any binding agreements or communication.

### Examples

#### The Prisoner's Dilemma
This is the most famous example of a Nash Equilibrium.

- **Scenario:** Two prisoners are interrogated separately. Each can either "Betray" (defect) or "Remain Silent" (cooperate).
- **Outcomes:**
    - If both remain silent: 1 year in prison each.
    - If one betrays and the other remains silent: The betrayer goes free, the silent one gets 10 years.
    - If both betray: 5 years each.
- **The Nash Equilibrium:** Both prisoners choose to **Betray**. Why? From Prisoner A's perspective: "If Prisoner B is silent, I get 1 year if I stay silent, or 0 years if I betray (better). If Prisoner B betrays, I get 10 years if I stay silent, or 5 years if I betray (better)." No matter what the other does, *Betraying* is always the best individual choice. Therefore, at the equilibrium (Betray, Betray), neither can improve their outcome by changing alone.

#### Traffic Flow (Left vs. Right)
- **Scenario:** Two cars approach each other on a narrow road. Each driver can swerve "Left" or "Right."
- **Outcomes:**
    - If both go Left: They miss each other (Good).
    - If both go Right: They miss each other (Good).
    - If one goes Left and the other goes Right: They crash (Bad).
- **The Nash Equilibria:** There are two pure-strategy equilibria: (Left, Right) and (Right, Left). If you are going Left and the other is going Right, you crash. If you are going Left and they are going Left, you both survive. The stable states are where you both match (either both go left or both go right). This explains why society needs a driving convention.

#### The Tipping Game (Audience Stand-up)
- **Scenario:** A concert audience decides when to stand up.
- **Outcome:** If everyone stands, no one sees better, but everyone is uncomfortable. If everyone sits, people behind can see (best collective outcome). However, an individual can always see better by standing up, as long as the person in front of them is sitting.
- **The Nash Equilibrium:** Everyone eventually stands. The first person to stand gets a better view, forcing the person behind them to stand to see. This cascade continues until everyone is standing, even though it is a worse outcome for the group than if everyone had stayed seated.

### Related Mental Models

Understanding the Nash Equilibrium helps contextualize many other decision-making concepts.

- [[Game Theory]] - The broader field of study analyzing strategic interactions. The Nash Equilibrium is its most fundamental concept.
- [[Prisoner's Dilemma]] - A specific game structure that often results in a sub-optimal Nash Equilibrium for the group.
- [[Zero-Sum Game]] - A game where one player's gain is another's loss. The Nash Equilibrium in zero-sum games is often found using a [[Minimax]] strategy.
- [[Tragedy of the Commons]] - A scenario where individual rational behavior (seeking personal gain) leads to the depletion of a shared resource. This is a Nash Equilibrium where the collective outcome is disastrous.
- [[Pareto Efficiency]] - A state where no one can be made better off without making someone else worse off. The Nash Equilibrium is *not* always Pareto efficient (as seen in the Prisoner's Dilemma).
- [[Evolutionary Stable Strategy]] - A refinement of the Nash Equilibrium used in evolutionary biology and [[Behavioral Economics]]. It describes a strategy that, if adopted by a population, cannot be invaded by a mutant strategy.
- [[Bounded Rationality]] - A concept that challenges the "perfect rationality" assumption behind the classic Nash Equilibrium, suggesting humans often settle for "satisficing" rather than optimizing.
