# NW (Network Effects)

## Definition

**Network Effects** (also known as *demand-side economies of scale*) occur when a product or service becomes more valuable as more people use it. The core idea is that each new user adds value for all existing users, creating a self-reinforcing growth loop. This phenomenon is a powerful **moat** in business and a key driver of platform dominance.

Network effects can be direct (e.g., telephones — more users = more connections possible) or indirect (e.g., an app store — more users attract more developers, which in turn makes the platform more valuable to users).

## Examples

- **Social Networks:** Facebook, LinkedIn, and Twitter become more useful as your friends and colleagues join.
- **Marketplaces:** eBay, Airbnb, and Uber benefit from more buyers attracting more sellers, and vice versa.
- **Communication Platforms:** WhatsApp, Zoom, and Slack gain value when a critical mass of your contacts uses them.
- **Operating Systems:** Windows and macOS have more third‑party software and hardware support because of their large user base.
- **Payment Systems:** PayPal and Venmo become more convenient as more merchants and individuals accept them.

## Related Mental Models

- [[Metcalfe’s Law]] – The value of a network is proportional to the square of the number of connected users (n²).
- [[Critical Mass]] – The threshold at which a network effect becomes self‑sustaining.
- [[Virality]] – A growth mechanism often fueled by network effects, where users promote the product to others.
- [[Lock‑In Effect]] – High switching costs that cement the advantage of an established network.
- [[The Tipping Point]] – The moment when a network crosses the critical mass and accelerates.
- [[Economies of Scale]] – The supply‑side counterpart to network effects, where per‑unit costs drop as production scales.
- [[Winner‑Take‑All Market]] – Markets with strong network effects tend to consolidate around a single dominant player.
- [[Chicken‑and‑Egg Problem]] – The challenge of initially attracting both sides of a multi‑sided network.
