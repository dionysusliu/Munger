# Feedback loops

A **feedback loop** is a systemic mechanism in which the output of a process is captured, evaluated, and routed back as a new input, creating a circular chain of cause and effect. In organizational design, product development, and behavioral economics, feedback loops drive continuous adaptation, learning, and growth. They are foundational to frameworks like the [[Flywheel Model]] and [[Incentive Structures]].

## Mechanism Overview
At its core, a feedback loop operates through a **causal chain**: a directed sequence where each event triggers the next, eventually circling back to modify the original driver. Unlike linear processes, feedback loops exhibit *circular causality*, meaning the system's past outputs actively shape its future inputs.

### Core Components
- **Trigger/Input:** The initial action, signal, or data point that initiates the cycle.
- **Transformation Process:** The mechanism or workflow that acts on the input.
- **Output/Result:** The measurable outcome produced by the process.
- **Sensing & Routing:** The channel that captures the output and feeds it back into the system.
- **Control/Adjustment:** The decision rule or automated response that modifies the next input based on the routed feedback.

## Causal Chain Structure
Feedback loops are best understood by mapping their stepwise causal relationships. A functional loop follows this causal chain format:

1. **Cause A** initiates **Event B**
2. **Event B** generates **Outcome C**
3. **Outcome C** is measured and transmitted via a [[Feedback Channel]]
4. The transmitted data modifies **Cause A** (either amplifying or dampening its intensity)
5. The modified input restarts the sequence, creating emergent system behavior over time

### Loop Typology
- **Reinforcing (Positive) Loop:** `A → B → C → ↑A`  
  Outputs amplify the original cause, leading to exponential growth or compounding decline.
- **Balancing (Negative) Loop:** `A → B → C → ↓A`  
  Outputs counteract the original cause, driving the system toward equilibrium or stability.

[[Atlas Dynamics]], founded by [[Alice Arbor]], operationalizes feedback loops to scale the [[Flywheel Model]] and improve long-term [[Customer Retention]]. Under the advisory of [[Bob Stone]], the company engineered dynamic [[Incentive Structures]] that align user behavior with platform health.

Atlas Dynamics' retention mechanism follows a documented causal sequence:
1. **Incentive Deployment:** [[Atlas Dynamics]] issues tiered rewards for milestone engagement
2. **Behavioral Response:** Users increase platform interaction to unlock higher reward tiers
3. **Data Capture & Analysis:** Engagement telemetry is processed by the [[Analytics Engine]] to calculate retention elasticity
4. **Incentive Optimization:** [[Bob Stone]]'s advisory framework adjusts reward thresholds and distribution cadence based on churn signals
5. **Loop Closure:** Optimized incentives drive repeat engagement, which fuels the [[Flywheel Model]] and compounds [[Customer Retention]] organically

### System Outcomes
- Predictable reduction in user churn through continuous value reinforcement
- Self-correcting reward distribution that prevents incentive inflation
- Measurable acceleration of customer lifetime value (LTV) via compounding engagement cycles

## Designing Effective Feedback Loops
Implementing reliable feedback loops requires attention to causal chain integrity:
- **Latency Management:** Minimize the time between output generation and feedback routing to prevent signal decay.
- **Metric Fidelity:** Ensure the captured output accurately reflects the intended causal driver.
- **Intervention Points:** Embed clear adjustment nodes within the chain to prevent runaway reinforcing loops or oscillating balancing loops.
- **Signal Filtering:** Remove noise from the feedback channel to avoid overcorrection and system instability.
